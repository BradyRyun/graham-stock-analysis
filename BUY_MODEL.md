Model 1: Regular Companies (Non-Financial)
Focuses heavily on cash generation and operational efficiency, using asset value as a safety floor.

30% | Free Cash Flow (FCF) Yield: Cash is king. This is the single most reliable indicator of actual profitability and shareholder return potential.

20% | Return on Invested Capital (ROIC): Proves management creates high-percentage returns on the money they deploy. Prevents buying stagnant businesses.

15% | Price-to-Earnings (P/E) Ratio: Provides standard earnings valuation context against the broader market.

15% | Price-to-Book (P/B) or BVPS: Acts as the margin of safety, but takes a back seat to cash and efficiency for asset-light businesses.

10% | Debt-to-Asset Ratio: The ultimate survival filter. A high score here penalizes and disqualifies heavily indebted companies.

5%  | Dividend / Yield Growth: Rewards sustainable, growing payout trajectories.

5%  | Sharpe Ratio: A minor tactical modifier to account for historical price volatility.

0%  | Graham Net-Net: Kept at zero by default because true "Net-Net" opportunities are incredibly rare for healthy, modern operating companies. (Note: If a stock actually qualifies as a Net-Net, this metric instantly overrides the model and becomes a strong buy).

Model 2: Financial Companies (Banks, Insurance, Funds)
Focuses heavily on asset integrity, capital efficiency, and leverage management.

30% | Price-to-Book (P/B) / BVPS: The primary valuation anchor for financial firms. Trading below 1.0 is a massive signal that requires immediate investigation.

25% | Return on Equity (ROE): Replaces ROIC for financials. It tells you if the firm is actually generating a profit on its book value. If P/B is low but ROE is high, it is a high-conviction buy.

20% | Debt-to-Asset Ratio: Crucial for financials, as they are highly leveraged by design. This monitors capital adequacy and insolvency risk.

15% | Price-to-Earnings (P/E) Ratio: Evaluates current core net interest and fee income against the price.

5%  | Dividend / Yield Growth: Financials are traditional income stocks; steady growth proves balance sheet stability.

5%  | Sharpe Ratio: Gauges market sentiment and volatility around the stock.

0%  | FCF Yield: Purposely excluded. "Free Cash Flow" formulas break down entirely on bank financial statements because moving cash around is their primary business operation.
